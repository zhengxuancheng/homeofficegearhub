## What to Look For

### Microphone type: condenser vs. dynamic

Condenser microphones are the standard for home office use. They are sensitive, capture a wide frequency range, and produce clear, detailed audio. The trade-off is that they pick up more background noise — keyboard clicks, room echo, AC hum. Dynamic microphones are less sensitive and reject background noise better, but they require the mic to be closer to your mouth and often need more gain (volume boost). For a quiet home office, a condenser mic gives you the best audio quality. For a noisy environment, go dynamic.

### USB vs. XLR

USB microphones plug directly into your computer — no mixer, no audio interface, no extra cables. This is the right choice for almost every home office user. XLR microphones require an audio interface and are used in professional studio settings. The audio quality difference between a good USB mic and a budget XLR setup is negligible for meetings, podcasts, and voiceovers. Don't overthink this.

### Polar pattern: cardioid, omnidirectional, bidirectional

Cardioid is the standard pattern for voice — it picks up sound from the front of the mic and rejects sound from the sides and back. This minimizes keyboard noise and room echo. Omnidirectional picks up sound from all directions (good for conference calls with multiple people in the room but picks up everything). Bidirectional picks up from front and back (for two-person interviews). For 95% of home office use, a cardioid pattern is what you want.

### Headset vs. standalone mic

Headsets with a built-in boom microphone are convenient — one device handles audio in and out. The mic is always at the same position, and there is no extra equipment on your desk. Standalone microphones deliver better audio quality but require headphones or earbuds for listening, and they take up desk space. If audio quality matters most, go standalone. If convenience matters most, go headset.

## FAQ

### Do I really need a dedicated microphone if my laptop has a built-in one?

Yes. Laptop microphones are designed for voice dictation at close range, not for meetings or recording. They sound hollow, pick up every keyboard click, and echo in any room with hard surfaces. A $30 USB mic sounds better than the microphone in a $3000 laptop. The improvement is not subtle — others will notice the difference before you do.

### Can I use a gaming headset for work calls?

Most gaming headsets prioritize game audio (bassy, exaggerated) over voice clarity. They work for calls but sound less natural than a dedicated USB mic or a professional headset. The Logitech Zone Wireless 2 and similar "professional" headsets use different tuning that optimizes voice clarity over dramatic audio effects.

### USB or XLR — which sounds better?

At the same price point, a USB microphone sounds as good as an XLR microphone with a budget audio interface. The difference is not in audio quality but in flexibility: XLR lets you upgrade components individually, add hardware compression, and use multiple mics. For a single user at a desk doing meetings and podcasting, USB is the right choice.

### How do I reduce room echo with a condenser mic?

Three fixes, in order of effectiveness: 1) Move closer to the mic (2-4 inches instead of 6-8 inches reduces room capture by half). 2) Add soft surfaces to your room — a rug, curtains, acoustic foam behind the monitor. 3) Use a dynamic mic if your room is highly reverberant. Software noise gates help but do not eliminate echo.

### What is gain staging and why does it matter?

Gain staging means setting the microphone input level so your voice hits the optimal range without clipping (distortion) or being too quiet. Aim for peaks around -6 dB to -3 dB in your recording software. Too low and you will hear noise when you boost the volume. Too high and you get digital distortion. Most USB mics have a gain knob — adjust it while speaking at your normal volume until the levels look right.
