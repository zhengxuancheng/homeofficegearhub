## What to Look For

### Surface Quality

The surface material determines how long the board stays usable. Porcelain-enameled steel is the gold standard — it resists ghosting (the faint leftover marks that won't fully erase), cleans easily with just water, and lasts for years. Melamine (resin-coated) is cheaper but ghosts within weeks of daily use. Glass boards look premium and never ghost, but they're heavier and the markers feel different (glass is non-porous, so ink sits on top rather than absorbing slightly). Adhesive film whiteboards stick to your wall — they're usable but pick up dust and lose their adhesive over time.

### Size and Mounting

For a desk-adjacent board, 18x24 inches covers most planning needs without dominating your workspace. Wall-mounted boards save desk space but require commitment. Desktop easels and folding boards are flexible — move them to your desk when you need them, put them away when you don't. Rolling boards give you the most surface area without wall damage but take up floor space.

### Magnetic vs. Non-Magnetic

Magnets add significant utility. You can hang papers, checklists, photos, and notes alongside your writing. A magnetic board becomes the hub for reference materials. Non-magnetic boards are fine for pure writing, but you lose the ability to pin things up. If you're choosing between two similar boards, get the magnetic one.

### Wet-Erase vs. Dry-Erase

Dry-erase markers work on all whiteboard surfaces and erase with a dry cloth. The common problem: dry-erase ink dries out quickly if left on the board, and can stain porous surfaces if left for more than a few days. Wet-erase markers (like the ones used in restaurants for menu boards) require a damp cloth to erase and don't dry out on the board. They're better for permanent or semi-permanent content like weekly planning grids. Some people prefer wet-erase for structured layouts and dry-erase for day-to-day notes.

## FAQ

### What's the best size whiteboard for a home office?

For task management and weekly planning, 14x20 to 18x24 inches is enough. For project planning, mind mapping, or creative work, go with 24x36 or larger. The right size also depends on how close you sit to it — a 14x20 on your desk works fine for daily tasks but is too small for anything that involves sticky notes and arrows. Bigger is generally better if you have the wall space.

### How do I remove ghosting from a whiteboard?

For melamine boards, isopropyl alcohol (70% or higher) on a microfiber cloth removes most ghosting. For stubborn marks, whiteboard cleaner spray or a small amount of hand sanitizer works. For glass boards, ghosting isn't an issue — they clean completely with just water. If your melamine board ghosts permanently after repeated cleaning, the surface has degraded and it's time to replace it.

### Glass vs. melamine whiteboard — which is better?

Glass wins on surface quality and longevity — it never ghosts, erases perfectly, and looks better. The downsides are weight, price, and installation difficulty. Melamine wins on affordability and weight. For a permanent wall installation where you can spend $150-200, glass is worth the upgrade. For a board that might move or get replaced, melamine is practical.

### Can I use a whiteboard for digital hybrid planning?

Yes. Take a photo of your whiteboard at the end of each week with your phone. Some people use scanning apps (Adobe Scan, Microsoft Lens) that crop and straighten the board. The Wacom Bamboo Slate handles this automatically but costs more. For the physical/digital hybrid workflow, a glass or porcelain surface with a weekly photo archive gives you the best of both without the smart board investment.

### How do I clean a whiteboard properly?

Use a microfiber cloth and either water (for fresh marks and glass boards) or isopropyl alcohol (for dried marks on melamine). Don't use abrasive cleaners, paper towels (they leave lint), or household glass cleaners with ammonia (they degrade the board surface over time). Clean the board weekly even if it looks clean — ink residue builds up invisibly and causes ghosting later. Replace your markers when they start drying out — dried markers are the main cause of staining.
