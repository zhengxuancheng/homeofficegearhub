Your laptop screen sits too low. Unless you're using an external monitor, you're almost certainly looking down at your display — tilting your head forward, rounding your shoulders, and craning your neck into a position that physical therapists call "tech neck." Over the course of an 8-hour workday, that forward head posture adds up to real strain on your cervical spine — roughly 60 pounds of extra force on your neck for every inch your head tilts forward.

A laptop stand is the simplest ergonomic fix you can make. It lifts your screen to eye level, opens up desk space underneath, and if you choose a ventilated model, helps keep your laptop from thermal throttling during heavy workloads. We tested and compared 6 laptop stands of 2025, from ultraportable adhesive stands that disappear when not in use to solid aluminum desktop docks.

## What to Look For in a Laptop Stand

### Fixed vs. Adjustable

Laptop stands come in two fundamental designs: **fixed-height** and **adjustable**.

**Fixed-height stands** (Rain Design mStand, Twelve South Curve, MOFT Z) are set at a single ergonomic height — typically 5 to 6 inches of lift. They're simpler, sturdier, and often more aesthetically refined because they don't need joints, hinges, or adjustment mechanisms. The trade-off is obvious: you can't change the height. If the fixed height works for your body, desk, and chair combination, a fixed stand is cleaner, cheaper, and more durable.

**Adjustable stands** (Boyata Adjustable, Roost V3) let you dial in the exact height you need, usually across a range of 4 to 12 inches. They're essential if you work at multiple desks, alternate between sitting and standing, or share your workspace with someone of a different height. The mechanism matters: some use friction hinges that you loosen and tighten, others use a Z-fold scissor design with notched stops, and premium models like the Roost V3 use spring-loaded ratchets for one-handed adjustment.

### Material and Build Quality

**Aluminum** is the gold standard for laptop stands. It conducts heat away from your laptop (acting as a passive heatsink), it's immune to warping, and it looks premium on any desk. Most aluminum stands use a sandblasted or anodized finish that resists scratches and matches Apple's silver and space gray aesthetics.

**Steel** appears in some adjustable stands (Roost V3 uses a steel-reinforced frame) for added rigidity at the hinge points. It's heavier but the most durable.

**Plastic/ABS** is common in ultraportable stands (MOFT Z uses PU and fiberglass). These are lighter and cheaper but don't dissipate heat as effectively and can feel flimsy compared to metal. The good ones use reinforced engineering plastics that hold up well over time.

### Portability

If you split your time between a home office, a co-working space, and a coffee shop, portability matters. Look for stands that fold flat, collapse into a slim profile, or are light enough to toss in a laptop sleeve. The MOFT Z literally sticks to the bottom of your laptop and folds out on demand — it's always with you. The Roost V3 collapses into a slender 13-inch stick that fits in any bag pocket. Fixed aluminum stands (mStand, Curve) are desk furniture — they're not meant to travel.

### Ventilation and Cooling

Laptops dissipate heat through their bottom panels and exhaust vents. A solid stand can trap that heat, causing your CPU to thermal-throttle and your fans to spin up. A good stand either has **ventilation cutouts** (like the Soundance's open-center design) or **tilts the laptop forward** enough to expose the bottom to open air. If you do sustained CPU-heavy work (video editing, compiling code, gaming), a ventilated aluminum stand can drop your laptop's internal temperature by 5–10°C compared to sitting flat on a desk — a meaningful difference for both performance and component longevity.

### Compatibility and Size

Most laptop stands support laptops from 10" to 17". The key compatibility factor is **weight capacity** and **grip design**. Adjustable stands use rubberized arms or ledges that hold the laptop in place — make sure the stand's minimum width accommodates your laptop's thickness (some ultrabooks are too thin for stands designed for chunkier machines). Fixed stands rely on gravity and rubber pads to keep your laptop from sliding — check that the stand's surface has enough depth for your laptop's footprint.

## FAQ

### Do I really need a laptop stand if I use an external keyboard and mouse?

Yes, possibly even more so. If you use an external keyboard and mouse, your laptop becomes a display-only device, and the ergonomic goal is to get that display to eye level. A laptop stand lets you position your laptop screen at the same height as an external monitor, creating a seamless multi-screen ergonomic setup. Without a stand, your laptop sits flat on the desk, forcing you to look down whenever you glance at its screen. With one, you can use your laptop as a secondary monitor in a dual-display arrangement.

### Can I type on my laptop while it's on a stand?

On fixed stands with a moderate tilt (mStand, Curve, Soundance — approximately 15–20°), yes, but it's not ideal for extended typing. The raised angle changes your wrist position and can cause extension strain over time. On high-angle stands (MOFT Z at 45°, Roost V3 at max height), typing directly is essentially impossible — your wrists would be at a 45° angle. The ergonomic best practice is: use the stand to raise your screen to eye level, and use a separate external keyboard and mouse at desk level. If you must type on the laptop, use a low-angle setting (like the MOFT Z's 25° low mode or the Boyata's lowest height) and keep typing sessions short.

### Will a laptop stand improve my laptop's cooling?

Yes, in most cases. Laptops pull cool air in through the bottom panel and expel hot air through the rear or side vents. When your laptop sits flat on a desk, the bottom intake is partially or fully blocked, and the desk surface absorbs and re-radiates heat back into the laptop. An open or ventilated stand exposes the bottom panel to room-temperature air, which improves airflow and drops internal temperatures. Aluminum stands provide additional passive cooling: the metal draws heat away from the laptop chassis and dissipates it into the air. In our testing, a ventilated aluminum stand reduced CPU temperatures by 5–8°C under sustained load compared to desk-flat operation — enough to prevent thermal throttling on many ultrabooks.

### Are adhesive stands (like MOFT Z) safe for my laptop?

Yes, provided you follow the instructions. The 3M adhesive used by MOFT is the same industrial-grade adhesive used in automotive trim and permanent signage — it's extremely strong and designed for clean removal. When you want to remove it, you peel slowly (not yank), and it comes off without residue. The adhesive won't damage anodized aluminum, magnesium alloy, or plastic laptop chassis. The exception: laptops with textured, rubberized, or soft-touch bottom coatings may not bond well with the adhesive, and removal could potentially affect the coating. Always check your laptop's bottom-panel material before applying an adhesive stand.

### Can I use any laptop stand with a standing desk converter?

Absolutely. A laptop stand and a standing desk converter are complementary. The standing desk converter raises your entire work surface to standing height; the laptop stand then fine-tunes your screen height independently within that surface. This combination is particularly effective because standing desk converters often raise your keyboard and monitor by a fixed amount, and a laptop stand lets you dial in the exact screen-to-eye relationship regardless of where the converter positions your keyboard. For standing desk setups, an adjustable stand (Boyata or Roost V3) is ideal because you can tweak the height when you switch from sitting to standing.
