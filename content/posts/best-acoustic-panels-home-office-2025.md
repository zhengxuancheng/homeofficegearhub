If you've ever been on a Zoom call that sounded like you were broadcasting from a bathroom, you know the problem. Hard surfaces — walls, desks, floors, windows — reflect sound. Your voice bounces around, comes back at the microphone a few milliseconds later, and the result is that hollow, echoey, "I'm definitely working from home" quality that everyone on the call can hear but nobody mentions.

Acoustic panels fix this. They absorb sound rather than reflecting it, which means less echo, less reverb, and a voice that sounds clear and present rather than distant and cavernous. I tested 7 acoustic panel setups in a untreated 10×12 foot home office with hardwood floors and a large window — the sort of room that's absolutely punishing for audio.

## How We Tested

Each panel setup was evaluated on:

- **Sound absorption** — Measured reverberation time (RT60) before and after installation using a calibrated measurement mic
- **Call quality improvement** — Recorded test calls and played them back blind to three listeners
- **Installation difficulty** — Time and frustration to mount each system
- **Aesthetics** — Let's be honest, most acoustic panels are ugly. Some manage to look decent
- **Coverage area needed** — How many panels to achieve a meaningful improvement
- **Fire rating** — Class A is essential for any indoor treatment

## FAQ

### How many acoustic panels do I need for my home office?

For a typical 10×12 foot home office with standard 8-foot ceilings, you'll want to cover 15–25% of the total wall surface area. That's about 30–50 square feet of panel coverage — roughly 4–6 of the 2×4 foot panels. Start by treating the wall behind your desk (the one your microphone faces) and the wall directly opposite. Those two walls handle the primary sound reflection path. If you still hear echo after that, add panels to the side walls at ear height where sound reflects from the nearest wall to your microphone.

### Where should I place acoustic panels in my home office?

The most important panel goes behind your monitor, on the wall you face while talking. This kills the primary reflection — your voice hits the wall, bounces back, and hits your microphone a few milliseconds after the direct sound. The second most important location is the wall behind you. Your voice reflects off that wall, bounces across the room, and arrives at your microphone late and degraded. After those two positions, add panels to the side walls at the reflection point — roughly where a mirror placed on the wall would show your speaker's mouth.

### What's the difference between acoustic panels and soundproofing?

Acoustic panels absorb sound within the room; they don't block it from leaving or entering. They reduce echo, reverb, and room coloration but won't stop your neighbor from hearing you. Soundproofing requires mass-loaded vinyl, decoupled wall construction, sealed gaps around doors and windows — major structural work. Acoustic panels improve call quality. Soundproofing keeps your conversation private. If you want both, you need both.

### Can I DIY acoustic panels?

Yes, and it's not difficult. A 2×4 foot DIY panel costs about $20–$30 in materials: a sheet of rigid fiberglass or mineral wool (like Owens Corning 703 or Rockwool Safe'n'Sound), a frame made from 1×3 lumber, and acoustically transparent fabric. The total cost is about 40–50% less than buying pre-made panels. The trade-off is time (about 30 minutes per panel once you have a process) and edge finish — DIY panels won't look as polished as factory-wrapped ones unless you're good with a staple gun.

### Do acoustic panels help with background noise from outside?

Marginally, but not in the way most people hope. Acoustic panels absorb sound that's already inside the room; they don't block sound from entering. If your neighbor's TV or street traffic is the problem, you need soundproofing, not acoustic treatment. What acoustic panels can do is reduce the reverb of whatever background noise does enter the room, making it less noticeable in the overall sound field. The distinction matters — panels clean up the sound that's already there, but they don't silence external noise.

### Are fabric-wrapped panels better than foam?

Yes, for most purposes. Fabric-wrapped fiberglass panels have a higher NRC (0.90+) across a wider frequency range than foam (typically 0.30–0.50 for basic wedge foam). Fiberglass absorbs down to about 125Hz with sufficient thickness, while foam starts losing effectiveness below 500Hz. Foam is cheaper, lighter, and easier to mount, but the sound absorption per dollar of fiberglass panels is significantly better. For a home office where call clarity matters, fiberglass is the right choice. For a bedroom recording of voice memos, foam is fine.
